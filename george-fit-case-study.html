Redesigning George's Financial Health Screen

George's Financial Health screen shows three identical red warnings with no priority order and no clear next step, leaving users overwhelmed instead of informed. This project redesigns that screen to reduce cognitive load, surface one actionable priority, and replace judgment-heavy language with guidance that actually helps users move forward.

Scope
Single screen redesign
Platform
George iOS App
My Role
UX / UI Designer
User Context
4 years daily use
00 · Starting Point

Why this screen, why now

George is a polished banking app: clear navigation, solid transactions, smooth Invest tab. But one screen has always felt off: Financial Health (FIT).

I've opened it dozens of times and walked away feeling worse, not because my finances are bad, but because of how the information is presented.

"The app told me I was lacking essential protection and not investing at all, on the same screen, at the same visual weight. I didn't know what to do first, so I closed it."

What George does well

Transactions, account overview, and the Invest tab are well-structured. The dark UI is clean and consistent.

The one thing it gets wrong

Every problem gets the same warning icon, no priority is set, and there's no next step. Users leave overwhelmed, not informed.

01 · Problem Definition

Three problems in one screen

Three problems compound each other and make genuinely useful data feel frustrating.

  • 1

    Alarm fatigue through equal visual weight

    Three out of four items show the same warning icon. When everything looks urgent, users ignore all of it.

  • 2

    Negative framing with no action pathway

    "You are lacking essential protection." Statements like this are judgments, not guidance. There's no next step, no sense of effort required.

  • 3

    No sense of overall health or progress

    No score, no summary, no overall signal. Users hit a list of problems with no context. Without a "you are here" anchor, everything feels worse than it is.

01.5 · Key Insights

How people actually use financial data

Why financial screens frustrate users, even when the data is correct.

⚡

People scan, they don't read

Users scan for a signal in under three seconds. If four cards need reading to understand their situation, they'll close the screen. They don't come back.

🧠

Too many warnings is the same as no warnings

When every item is flagged, none feel urgent. Show one clear next step, not a ranked list of problems.

💬

Language shapes how safe people feel

"You are lacking" reads as criticism. Users already feel anxious about money; a judgmental screen makes engagement less likely. Tone is a design decision.

🎯

Context before detail

A score or summary first makes everything that follows feel manageable. Going straight to a list of problems puts users on the defensive immediately.

04 · Wireframes

Structure before visuals

Structural questions first: what's the most important element? What does the user see in the first second? How many decisions can we remove before they hit any problem framing?

Grey boxes only. No colour, no icons. Pure hierarchy.

Before · Current structure
Issue: All four rows identical in height, icon size, and visual weight. No orientation element. Ads placed immediately after problems.
After · Redesigned structure
Solution: Score anchors the screen. Priority card takes 2× the space of list items. Compact rows below keep full picture accessible without overwhelming.

06 · Iteration

How the redesign evolved across three rounds

Three rounds to find the right balance between information and simplicity.

Version 1
Full dashboard replacement

Replaced the list with a pie chart, four score dials, and progress graphs. Visually impressive, but introduced new complexity.

Removed
Pie chart 4 score dials Progress graphs
Problem

Too much at once, just styled differently.

Version 2
Score + reworded list

One score at the top, rewritten list below. Better tone, but users still didn't know where to start.

Added
Health score Positive language
Still missing
Priority signal Inline action
Problem

Score helped orientation but didn't answer "what do I do first?"

Version 3 Final
Score + priority card + compact list

Added a "Start Here" card between score and list. Three layers (orient, prioritise, explore) solved both problems at once.

Added
Priority card Inline CTA 3-tier status system Area detail view
Why it works

"What do I do?" is answered at a glance. Everything else is available but not competing.

What iteration revealed

The problem wasn't too many elements; it's that all were treated as equally urgent. Version 3 has the same data points, structured so users encounter them in the right order.

07 · Design System

New components, existing system

Each new component slots into George's existing system and is reusable beyond this screen.

🎯

Health Score Ring

Circular progress indicator. Colour-coded by range. Always the first thing seen.

⭐

Priority Card

Surfaces one recommended action with an inline CTA. Only shown when there's a genuine priority.

🔵

3-State Status Indicator

Dot + label replacing binary warning icons: On track / In progress / Not started. No more alarm-or-nothing.

📋

Area Detail View

Progress bar, plain-language explanation, numbered steps. Built on existing George components.

What's preserved

Dark background, card radius, typography, navigation, and blue accent are all unchanged. The redesign evolves the system, it doesn't replace it.

08 · Outcome

A screen users actually want to open

Three friction points removed through structure, not decoration.

📊

Clearer understanding of spending

Score and category breakdown give users a clear picture without asking them to interpret four warning states.

⚡

Faster decision-making

One action instead of four. Less time deciding, more time acting.

🧠

Reduced cognitive load

Score first, priority second, full list third. Users process only what's needed at each step.

Reflection

The best redesigns don't add, they clarify. This screen didn't need more features, it needed better structure and a more human tone.

Background

Industrial design taught me that quality is how something makes you feel, not just how it looks. The same applies here.

What I'd validate next

I'd test whether the priority card gets followed or dismissed. I'd also check whether a score creates anxiety for users in genuinely poor financial health.
